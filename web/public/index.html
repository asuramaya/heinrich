model forensics · residual-stream interpretability

Measure what a model computes — not what it says.

Heinrich is an instrument for the inside of a language model. It captures the internal geometry — residual-stream projections, attention, neuron activations — and presents each measurement in its own lane, with no ground-truth calibration to smooth it over. Then it lets you fly through it in your browser.

what it finds

The output is confabulation. The computation is the signal.

A few results that fall out of measuring the residual stream directly, across a dozen models:

01 · variance
Safety is 0.5% of it
Of everything a model's residual stream encodes, language identity is ~10% and comply-vs-refuse is ~0.9%. Safety is a trace signal — 0.5% of displacement variance.
02 · neurons
Crystallization is one neuron
At certain layers a single MLP gate fires for nearly every token and collapses the representation to one axis. You can watch it dominate the field.
03 · decision
The first word decides
Safety routes through first-token selection — it pushes "Sorry" up and "Sure" down. Everything after the first word is confabulation.
04 · baseline
Silence isn't neutral
Every measurement is relative to a tilted baseline. One model's silence is maximum refusal; another's is mild compliance. Always check the baseline.

the principle

Measurement and calibration interfere. Heinrich captures state, not interpretation — each scorer and projection in its own isolated lane, no FPR/FNR tuning to round off the disagreements. The disagreement is the signal. Interpretation is left to the reader.
